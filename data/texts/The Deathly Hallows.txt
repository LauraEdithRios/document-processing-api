Long ago, in a distant land, there lived three brothers who were traveling along a lonely, winding road at twilight. As they journeyed, they came upon a deep and treacherous river. The water was dark and swift, and many who had tried to cross it before had lost their lives.

But the brothers were clever and skilled in magic. Instead of risking the crossing, they raised their wands and, together, cast a spell that created a bridge across the river. Just as they reached the middle of the bridge, a hooded figure appeared before them.

It was Death.

Death spoke to them in a cold, quiet voice. He was displeased, for the river had claimed many lives, and he had expected to take theirs as well. But being cunning, Death pretended to congratulate them. He said that each of them had earned a reward for their intelligence and bravery.

The eldest brother stepped forward first. He was a proud man, always seeking power above all else. He asked Death for a wand more powerful than any other in existence—a wand that would make him unbeatable in any duel. Death, though reluctant, fashioned a wand from a branch of an elder tree nearby and handed it to him. This became known as the Elder Wand.

The second brother then spoke. He was a man filled with sorrow, having lost someone he deeply loved. He asked for the power to bring back the dead. Death picked up a small stone from the riverbank and gave it to him. This became the Resurrection Stone.

Finally, the youngest brother stepped forward. He was humble and wise, and he did not trust Death. Instead of asking for power or the return of the dead, he asked for something that would allow him to leave that place without being followed. Death, growing irritated but bound by his promise, removed his own cloak of invisibility and handed it over. This became the Invisibility Cloak.

And so, the three brothers went their separate ways.

The eldest brother traveled far and wide, eager to prove the strength of his wand. In a distant village, he challenged another wizard to a duel and defeated him easily. That night, drunk with pride, he boasted loudly of his unbeatable wand. But while he slept, a thief crept into his room, slit his throat, and stole the Elder Wand. And so, Death took the first brother.

The second brother returned home and used the stone to bring back the one he had lost. She appeared before him, but she was pale and distant, as though separated from him by an invisible veil. Though she was there, she did not truly belong among the living. Filled with despair and longing, the second brother could not bear the pain. In the end, he took his own life to join her. And so, Death took the second brother.

But the youngest brother was different.

He hid under the cloak and lived a long and full life. Death searched for him everywhere, but could never find him. Years passed, and the brother grew old. Eventually, he chose to take off the cloak and pass it on to his son.

Then, at last, he greeted Death not as an enemy, but as an old friend. And together, they departed this life as equals.
